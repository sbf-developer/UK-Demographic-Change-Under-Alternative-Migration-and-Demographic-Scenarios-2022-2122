\begin{abstract}
\noindent
This paper presents the design of a transparent, reproducible population-projection system
for examining how the self-identified ethnic composition of the United Kingdom could evolve
over a 100-year horizon under alternative demographic assumptions. The system implements a
multistate cohort-component model with separate treatment of immigration, emigration, fertility
convergence (planned), partnership-weighted fertility inputs, and ethnic identification.
The central estimand is the \emph{conditional demographic trajectory} generated by a specified
set of assumptions---not a prediction of future population composition. Phase~1 establishes the
repository architecture, official data ingestion pipelines, ethnic category harmonisation for
England and Wales (Census 2021), and a minimal tested projection engine. All illustrative
outputs in this phase use clearly labelled placeholder parameters and must not be interpreted
as empirical projections. This is a \emph{methods and software design} contribution; empirical
calibration and historical validation are required before substantive long-run scenario analysis.
\end{abstract}

\vspace{1em}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} population projection, ethnicity, migration, cohort-component model,
United Kingdom, demographic scenarios

\section{Introduction}

Population projections by ethnic group are analytically important and methodologically
demanding. Ethnicity in the United Kingdom is measured through self-identification on the
census; categories have changed across census rounds; and the demographic behaviour associated
with ethnic groups---fertility, mortality, migration, partnership, and identity
transmission---differs in ways that standard national projections do not capture
\citep{haskey2002, rees2011, rees2017}.

Substantial prior work demonstrates both the feasibility and the sensitivity of long-run
ethnic composition trajectories to assumptions about fertility convergence and migration,
including \citet{wohland2010}, \citet{coleman2010}, and the \textsc{NewEthPop} system
\citep{newethpop}. This project builds on that tradition with an open-source pipeline
updated for Census 2021/2022 base populations, explicit preregistration of scenarios,
separate immigration and emigration modules, and planned ethnic identity transition
matrices estimated from census longitudinal change.

The research question is:

\begin{quote}
\emph{How does the projected ethnic composition of the United Kingdom change between 2022
and 2122 under alternative migration and demographic scenarios?}
\end{quote}

The answer is always conditional: under assumptions $A$, the model produces trajectory $T_A$.
The system never claims that ``the UK will have'' a particular ethnic composition.

\subsection{Scope and limitations (Phase 1)}

Phase~1 delivers the system architecture, data pipelines, harmonisation schema, and a
minimal tested projection engine. \textbf{No parameters are calibrated to UK data in this
phase.} The illustrative scenario uses placeholder values and is watermarked:

\begin{center}
\textcolor{red}{\textit{Methodological demonstration only. Not an empirical projection.}}
\end{center}

What Phase~1 can establish: the model structure is auditable and reproducible; Census 2021
ethnicity data for England and Wales are retrievable via official APIs; and the harmonisation
framework is specified. What it cannot yet establish: any empirical statement about future UK
ethnic composition, uncertainty quantification, or historical forecast accuracy.

\section{Conceptual framework}

\subsection{Ethnicity as self-identified and changeable}

Ethnicity is treated as a self-identified social category, distinct from nationality,
citizenship, religion, ancestry, and country of birth \citep{ons2021ethnicity, ons2023rm032}.
A person may report a different ethnic identity at different censuses. Country of birth is
never used as a direct proxy for ethnicity; where parental country of birth informs generation
classification, the mapping is empirically estimated and documented.

\subsection{Population state space}

The population at year $t$ is represented as:
\begin{equation}
    N_{n,e,g,s,a,t}
    \label{eq:state}
\end{equation}
where $n$ indexes UK nation, $e$ ethnic group, $g$ migrant generation, $s$ sex, and $a$ age
(0--100+). Generation categories are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item foreign-born, arrived as adult;
    \item foreign-born, arrived as child;
    \item UK-born with two foreign-born parents;
    \item UK-born with one foreign-born parent;
    \item UK-born with two UK-born parents.
\end{enumerate}
Generation is not inferred from ethnicity.

\subsection{Geographic scope}

England, Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland are modelled separately. Internal migration
flows sum to zero at UK level. Census base dates differ: England, Wales, and Northern
Ireland use Census 2021 (21 March 2021); Scotland uses Census 2022 (20 March 2022). A
harmonised mid-2022 base will adjust census-date counts using official mid-year population
estimates (Phase 2).

\section{Projection model}

\subsection{Cohort-component transition}

For existing cohorts, the core transition equation is:
\begin{equation}
    N_{n,e,g,s,a+1,t+1} = \sum_{e',g'} N_{n,e',g',s,a,t} \cdot S_{n,e',g',s,a,t} \cdot Q_{e' \rightarrow e,t} + I_{n,e,g,s,a,t} - O_{n,e,g,s,a,t} + M^{int}_{n,e,g,s,a,t}
    \label{eq:transition}
\end{equation}
where $S$ is survival probability, $Q$ is the ethnic-identification transition matrix
(operating on $e$ only in Phase 1), $I$ is immigration, $O$ is emigration, and
$M^{int}$ is net internal movement between UK nations. Deaths $D$ are implicit in
$(1-S)$ and appear explicitly in the accounting identity (Section~\ref{sec:accounting}).

\subsection{Birth model}

Births depend on maternal age-specific fertility rates (ASFR), not aggregate TFR alone:
\begin{equation}
    B_{n,e,g,s,t} = \sum_{a, e_m, g_m, e_p, g_p} N^{F}_{n,e_m,g_m,a,t} \cdot f_{n,e_m,g_m,a,t} \cdot P(e_p, g_p \mid e_m, g_m, n, t) \cdot P(e, g \mid e_m, e_p, g_m, g_p, n, t) \cdot P(s \mid t)
    \label{eq:births}
\end{equation}
where $P(s \mid t)$ is the sex ratio at birth. Children are not automatically assigned the
mother's ethnicity; $P(e, g \mid \cdot)$ is estimated from census parental-ethnicity tables
(Phase 7) or specified as a scenario assumption. In Phase 1, placeholder child-identification
probabilities are used and labelled accordingly.

Partnership affects fertility inputs only: the model does not include a partnership state
dimension. Two-sex couple formation is deferred to Phase 7.

\subsection{Fertility convergence (planned)}

Group-specific fertility will converge toward the national schedule:
\begin{equation}
    f_{e,g,a,t} = f^{UK}_{a,t} + \lambda_{e,g,t}\left(f^{initial}_{e,g,a} - f^{UK}_{a,t}\right), \quad \lambda_{e,g,t} = \exp(-\kappa_{e,g}\, d)
    \label{eq:convergence}
\end{equation}
where $d$ is duration of residence in the UK. Six specifications are planned; Phase 1 applies
static ASFR only.

\subsection{Migration}

Immigration and emigration are modelled separately at nation level:
\begin{equation}
    M^{net}_{n,e,g,s,a,t} = I_{n,e,g,s,a,t} - O_{n,e,g,s,a,t}
    \label{eq:netmigration}
\end{equation}
Migration scenarios specify measurable parameters: volumes, age and sex distributions,
ethnic composition, country of birth, entry route, and subsequent emigration probability.
Historical calibration will use admin-based migration estimates (ABME) from 2020 onward,
with IPS/LTIM used for earlier periods subject to documented comparability breaks
\citep{ons2024abme}.

\subsection{Harmonisation}

Two parallel ethnic classifications are maintained: a five-group broad classification
(\texttt{white}; \texttt{mixed}; \texttt{asian}; \texttt{black}; \texttt{other}) and a
detailed classification using harmonisable Census 2021 England/Wales categories. A
version-controlled mapping table records source country, census year, original category,
API codes, harmonised category, mapping rationale, comparability quality, and uncertainty
flag. Scotland, Northern Ireland, and historical census years are pending (Phases 4--6).

\section{Scenario design and uncertainty}

Scenarios are defined in YAML configuration files specifying measurable parameters.
Policy scenarios modify migration volumes, route shares, and composition---not vague labels
such as ``strict'' or ``liberal.''

Two modes are planned:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Deterministic:} explicit assumptions produce exact conditional trajectories.
    \item \textbf{Probabilistic (Phase 5):} parameters drawn from $\theta^{(r)} \sim p(\theta)$
    with $\geq 2{,}000$ Monte Carlo draws \citep{ons2024npp}.
\end{itemize}
Deterministic scenario ranges are never called confidence intervals.

\section{Validation strategy}
\label{sec:accounting}

\subsection{Accounting validation}

Every projection year verifies:
\begin{equation}
    P_{t+1} = P_t + B_t - D_t + I_t - O_t + M^{int}_t
    \label{eq:accounting}
\end{equation}
where $D_t$ denotes deaths and $M^{int}_t$ denotes net internal migration (distinct symbols
throughout). Additional checks: non-negative counts, valid probabilities, ethnic shares
summing to one, and internal migration summing to zero at UK level.

\subsection{Historical validation}

Hindcasts from Census 2001 to 2011 and 2011 to 2021/2022 will be conducted before any
75- or 100-year outputs are reported. Metrics include MAE, RMSE, share error, and coverage.
MAPE is reported only for groups above a minimum population threshold.

\section{Phase 1 results: system demonstration}

The Phase~1 illustrative scenario uses a synthetic population of 1 million distributed
across England and Wales with approximate Census 2021 broad ethnic shares. All demographic
parameters are placeholders. Projections run to 2050 in the stored output (the research
design specifies 2122, but long-horizon outputs require validated parameters).

\section{What this study can and cannot say}

\textbf{Can say (Phase 1):}
\begin{itemize}
    \item The projection system architecture is specified, implemented, and tested.
    \item Census 2021 ethnicity data for England and Wales are retrievable with documented provenance.
    \item Under clearly labelled placeholder assumptions, the model satisfies the population
    accounting identity.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Cannot yet say:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item How the UK's ethnic composition will change by 2122.
    \item Which assumptions contribute most to uncertainty.
    \item Whether the model would have accurately projected Census 2011 or 2021.
    \item Nation-specific projections for Scotland and Northern Ireland.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

This paper presents the Phase~1 architecture of a population-projection system for conditional
ethnic demographic trajectories under alternative scenarios. The system treats ethnicity as
self-identified and changeable, models immigration and emigration separately, and requires
historical validation before long-run outputs. Empirical calibration, probabilistic uncertainty,
and full UK coverage follow in subsequent phases.

\section*{Data and code availability}

Source code, scenario configurations, harmonisation tables, and data manifests are available
in the project repository. Raw API responses are cached with SHA-256 checksums under
\texttt{data/raw/}. Census microdata are not used; all inputs are aggregate official tables
under the Open Government Licence v3.0.
