# Methodological Design

**Project:** UK Ethnic Demographic Change Under Alternative Migration and Demographic Scenarios, 2022–2122  
**Author:** Scott Brodie Forsyth  
**Version:** 0.1.0 (Phase 1)  
**Date:** July 2026

---

## 1. Research objective

This project develops a transparent, reproducible population-projection system that examines how the self-identified ethnic composition of the United Kingdom could evolve over a 100-year horizon under alternative, explicitly specified assumptions about international migration, fertility, mortality, partnership formation, and ethnic identification.

The central estimand is:

> The conditional demographic trajectory generated by a specified set of migration, fertility, mortality, partnership, and ethnic-identification assumptions.

These projections are **not predictions**. They are conditional simulations: given assumptions X, the model produces trajectory Y. The value lies in understanding demographic mechanisms and comparing scenarios, not in forecasting a single future.

## 2. Conceptual framework

### 2.1 Ethnicity as a social construct

Ethnicity is treated throughout as:

- **Self-identified** — the category a person selects on a census form
- **Socially and historically situated** — categories change across censuses (e.g. the addition of "Roma" in 2021)
- **Potentially changeable** — individuals may report different ethnic identities at different times
- **Distinct from** nationality, citizenship, religion, ancestry, and country of birth

Country of birth is never used as a direct proxy for ethnicity. Where proxies are unavoidable (e.g. parental country of birth for generation classification), the relationship is empirically estimated and documented.

### 2.2 Population state space

The population at time \(t\) is represented as:

\[
N_{n,e,g,s,a,t}
\]

where:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| \(n\) | UK nation (England, Wales, Scotland, Northern Ireland) |
| \(e\) | Ethnic group (broad or detailed classification) |
| \(g\) | Migrant generation (five categories) |
| \(s\) | Sex |
| \(a\) | Single year of age (0–100+) |
| \(t\) | Calendar year |

**Generation categories** (distinct from ethnicity):

1. Foreign-born, arrived as adult
2. Foreign-born, arrived as child
3. UK-born with two foreign-born parents
4. UK-born with one foreign-born parent
5. UK-born with two UK-born parents

Generation is not inferred from ethnicity. A person identified as "White British" may belong to any generation category.

### 2.3 Geographic scope

The four UK constituent countries are modelled separately. Internal migration flows between nations are signed (positive = inflow, negative = outflow) and sum to zero at UK level. UK totals are produced only after harmonisation of nation-specific classifications.

**Census base dates:**
- England, Wales, Northern Ireland: Census 2021 (21 March 2021)
- Scotland: Census 2022 (20 March 2022)

A harmonised mid-2022 base population adjusts census-date counts to mid-year using official mid-year population estimates (MYE).

## 3. Core projection model (Model A)

### 3.1 Cohort-component transition equation

For existing cohorts:

\[
N_{n,e,g,s,a+1,t+1} = \sum_{e',g'} N_{n,e',g',s,a,t} \cdot S_{n,e',g',s,a,t} \cdot Q_{e' \rightarrow e,t} + I_{n,e,g,s,a,t} - O_{n,e,g,s,a,t} + M^{int}_{n,e,g,s,a,t}
\]

where:

| Symbol | Component |
|--------|-----------|
| \(S\) | Age-specific survival probability |
| \(Q\) | Ethnic-identification transition matrix (ethnicity only in Phase 1) |
| \(I\) | International immigration |
| \(O\) | International emigration |
| \(M^{int}\) | Net internal movement between UK nations |

Deaths \(D\) are implicit in \((1-S)\) and appear in the accounting identity (Section 8.1).

### 3.2 Birth model

Births depend on maternal age-specific fertility, not aggregate total fertility rates alone:

\[
B_{n,e,t} = \sum_{a, e_m, g_m, e_p, g_p} N^{F}_{n,e_m,g_m,a,t} \cdot f_{n,e_m,g_m,a,t} \cdot P(e_p, g_p \mid e_m, g_m, a, n, t) \cdot P(e \mid e_m, e_p, g_m, g_p, n, t)
\]

Children are **not** automatically assigned the mother's ethnicity. The child-identification probability \(P(e \mid \cdot)\) is estimated from census parental-ethnicity tables or specified as a scenario assumption.

### 3.3 Fertility convergence

Group-specific fertility converges toward the national schedule over duration in the UK:

\[
f_{e,g,a,t} = f^{UK}_{a,t} + \lambda_{e,g,t} \left( f^{initial}_{e,g,a} - f^{UK}_{a,t} \right)
\]

\[
\lambda_{e,g,t} = \exp(-\kappa_{e,g} \cdot d)
\]

where \(d\) is duration of residence and \(\kappa_{e,g}\) controls convergence speed. Six specifications are planned: no convergence, rapid, moderate, slow, generation-specific (estimated), and complete convergence by the second generation.

### 3.4 Mortality

Baseline mortality uses official ONS age- and sex-specific life tables. Ethnic-group mortality differences are applied only where suitable evidence exists (ONS Health Index, academic studies). Where evidence is inadequate, a common national schedule is used with clearly labelled sensitivity variants.

### 3.5 Migration

Immigration and emigration are modelled separately. Net migration is a derived quantity:

\[
M^{net}_{n,e,g,s,a,t} = I_{n,e,g,s,a,t} - O_{n,e,g,s,a,t}
\]

Migration scenarios specify: total volumes, age and sex distributions, ethnic composition, country of birth, entry route (work, study, family, asylum, return migration), and subsequent emigration probability.

## 4. Model ensemble

| Model | Description | Status |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| **A** | Deterministic multistate cohort-component | Phase 1 (implemented) |
| **B** | Probabilistic cohort-component with parameter draws | Phase 5 |
| **C** | Alternative fertility convergence, identity, emigration | Phase 5 |
| **D** | Dynamic microsimulation (optional future extension) | Not planned for initial release |

Models are compared rather than selecting one that produces a preferred outcome.

## 5. Ethnic classifications

Two parallel classifications are maintained:

**Harmonised broad (5 groups):** White; Mixed or multiple; Asian; Black; Other.

**Detailed:** The most detailed harmonisable categories from each census, mapped via a version-controlled table with comparability quality flags.

The mapping table records: source country, census year, original category, harmonised category, mapping rationale, comparability quality, and uncertainty flag. "White British" is not assumed to have identical meaning across all UK nations.

## 6. Scenario design

Scenarios are defined in YAML configuration files specifying measurable parameters. Policy scenarios modify migration volumes, route shares, and composition — not vague labels like "strict" or "liberal."

Scenario families include:

- Migration volume (zero, low, ONS-aligned, medium, high, declining, cyclical, shock)
- Migration composition (observed, convergent, route-specific)
- Fertility (common, maintained differences, convergence variants, stochastic)
- Emigration (common, origin-specific, duration-specific, return migration)
- Identity (fixed, estimated transitions, increasing mixed identification)
- Mortality (common, group differences, convergence)

## 7. Uncertainty quantification

Two modes:

**Deterministic:** Explicit assumptions produce exact conditional trajectories.

**Probabilistic:** Parameters drawn from documented distributions \(\theta^{(r)} \sim p(\theta)\), with at least 2,000 Monte Carlo draws. Reported intervals (50%, 80%, 95%) are **not** called confidence intervals in the deterministic case.

Uncertainty sources are separated: demographic stochasticity, parameter uncertainty, migration-scenario uncertainty, structural model uncertainty, and ethnic-classification uncertainty.

## 8. Validation strategy

### 8.1 Accounting validation (every year)

\[
P_{t+1} = P_t + B_t - D_t + I_t - O_t + M^{int}_t
\]

Plus checks for: non-negative counts, probabilities in [0,1], ethnic shares summing to one, internal migration summing to zero at UK level.

### 8.2 Historical validation (hindcasts)

- 2001 → 2011
- 2011 → 2021/2022
- 2001 → 2021/2022

Metrics: MAE, RMSE, MAPE (avoided for very small groups), share error, coverage.

### 8.3 Benchmark validation

Aggregate age-sex projections compared against official ONS national population projections. An ONS-aligned scenario preserves official total population while using the model's internally projected ethnic distribution. This does not imply ONS endorsement of the ethnic breakdown.

## 9. Decomposition

Population change decomposed as:

\[
\Delta N_e = B_e - D_e + I_e - O_e + T_e
\]

where \(T_e\) represents net ethnic-identification transitions. Counterfactual decompositions (migration held at zero, fertility differences removed, etc.) are analytical tools, not policy recommendations.

## 10. Transparency safeguards

1. Primary scenarios preregistered before results inspection
2. Every parameter and transformation published
3. Results from all preregistered scenarios retained
4. Assumptions not chosen based on preferred outcomes
5. Observed data distinguished from assumptions in all outputs
6. Individual behaviour not inferred from group averages
7. Ethnic groups not described as homogeneous
8. Ethnicity not used as proxy for economic contribution
9. Religion and ethnicity kept separate
10. Data gaps reported directly
11. Alternative plausible specifications included
12. Complete code and configuration reproducible
13. Automated tests preventing silent category loss
14. Manual data decisions recorded
15. Adversarial methodological review invited

## 12. Limitations (Phase 1)

At this stage, the system demonstrates methodological architecture only. No parameters are calibrated to UK data. The illustrative scenario uses clearly labelled placeholders and must never be presented as an empirical projection. Historical validation, probabilistic uncertainty, and nation-specific results for Scotland and Northern Ireland are not yet available.
